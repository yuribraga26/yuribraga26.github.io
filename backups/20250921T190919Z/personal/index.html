Personal Journey

Resilience, MCID, and Patient Safety

The Injury

At fifteen, my world changed in an instant. What started as another day on the soccer field ended with a devastating femur fracture that would challenge everything I thought I knew about resilience, recovery, and the human spirit. The injury was severe—affecting the epiphysis and requiring extensive surgical intervention.

The months that followed were a blur of hospital visits, physical therapy sessions, and the slow, painful process of learning to walk again. But perhaps more challenging than the physical recovery was watching my dreams of playing competitive soccer seemingly slip away. The doctors were cautious, the prognosis uncertain, and the road ahead looked impossibly long.

Rebuilding & Moving to the U.S.

Recovery was just the beginning. Moving to the United States brought new challenges and opportunities. I carried the weight of expectations—the "Brazilian kid" who was supposed to excel at soccer. But my body wasn't the same, and neither was my relationship with the sport I had once loved unconditionally.

There were setbacks—moments when the old injury would remind me of its presence, times when I questioned whether I was pushing too hard or not hard enough. The cultural adjustment, combined with the physical and emotional recovery, created a perfect storm of challenges that tested my resolve in ways I had never imagined.

But through it all, I learned something invaluable: true strength isn't about avoiding failure or setbacks—it's about how you respond to them. Each small victory, each day of progress, became a building block in reconstructing not just my physical capabilities, but my entire approach to challenges and adversity.

Return & Growth

The moment that changed everything came during my senior year. Standing on the field as we won the state championship, I felt goosebumps wash over me—not just from the victory, but from the realization of how far I had come. The injury that had once seemed like the end of my story had actually become the catalyst for a deeper understanding of perseverance, teamwork, and the power of never giving up.

That championship wasn't just about soccer; it was about proving to myself that setbacks don't define us—our response to them does. The years of rehabilitation, the moments of doubt, the gradual rebuilding of confidence and skill had all led to this moment of triumph. But more importantly, they had taught me to reframe adversity as an opportunity for growth.

I learned that sometimes the most meaningful victories come not from avoiding challenges, but from facing them head-on and emerging stronger. This experience fundamentally changed how I approach problems, whether on the field, in the classroom, or in my professional work.

From Soccer to Patient Safety

My journey from that hospital bed to the soccer field championship taught me something profound about risk, benefit, and the importance of evidence-based decision making. Every medical intervention I underwent had to prove its worth—not through hope or assumption, but through clear evidence of meaningful improvement.

This experience forged my MCID-first philosophy: "prove benefit before risk." I learned firsthand that invasive procedures should only be pursued when there's robust evidence they will achieve meaningful clinical improvement. The null hypothesis for any invasive intervention should be that it will not reach MCID unless proven otherwise.

My injury taught me that the human cost of unnecessary procedures extends far beyond statistics—it affects real people, real families, real dreams. This understanding became the driving force behind my commitment to developing AI systems that prioritize patient safety and meaningful outcomes over algorithmic elegance or impressive metrics.

Today

Today, as I work on CareFuse and pursue my engineering career, I carry the lessons from that soccer field with me every day. My mission to protect patients from invasive procedures unlikely to reach MCID isn't just professional—it's deeply personal. I know what it means to face uncertainty, to weigh risks and benefits, and to hope for meaningful improvement.

Through my faith community, my engineering work, and my continued involvement in athletics, I strive to apply the same principles that guided my recovery: evidence-based decision making, unwavering commitment to meaningful outcomes, and the belief that true success comes from serving others and protecting those who are vulnerable.

Every algorithm I develop, every model I train, every prediction CareFuse makes is infused with the understanding that behind every data point is a human being with hopes, fears, and dreams. My goal is to ensure that technology serves humanity, not the other way around—and that patient safety and well-being remain at the center of every decision we make.

"The injury that once seemed like my greatest setback became the foundation for my life's mission: ensuring that every medical decision is grounded in evidence and prioritizes meaningful improvement."
— Yuri Braga
